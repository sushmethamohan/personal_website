# Research

My research sits at the intersection of RNA biology, developmental neuroscience, and cancer. I am broadly interested in how RNA regulatory mechanisms shape cell fate — and how their disruption drives tumor formation.

---

## Nonsense-Mediated mRNA Decay in Cancer

The **nonsense-mediated mRNA decay (NMD)** pathway is a conserved cellular surveillance system that degrades mRNAs containing premature stop codons, protecting cells from harmful truncated proteins. During my PhD at McGill University, I uncovered an unexpected role for NMD as a **tumor suppressor** in medulloblastoma.

I demonstrated that disruption of the NMD pathway in cerebellar granule neuron progenitors (cGNPs) leads to enhanced Sonic hedgehog (SHH) signaling and increased tumor aggressiveness in the SHH subgroup of medulloblastoma. Using genome-wide RNA-seq, molecular reporter assays, and in vivo genetic models, I identified specific NMD target transcripts that feed into the Shh pathway — establishing a novel regulatory axis between RNA surveillance and developmental signaling in the brain.

This work is currently in revision at *Developmental Cell* and opens a new avenue for understanding tumor-suppressive mechanisms in pediatric brain cancer.

## Medulloblastoma Biology

**Medulloblastoma** is the most common malignant pediatric brain tumor, arising in the cerebellum. It is classified into four molecular subgroups (WNT, SHH, Group 3, Group 4) with distinct cellular origins, biology, and clinical outcomes.

My research focuses primarily on the **SHH subgroup**, where dysregulated Sonic hedgehog signaling drives uncontrolled proliferation of granule neuron progenitors in the developing cerebellum. I use multiple complementary mouse model systems — including transgenic SHH medulloblastoma models and patient-derived xenografts — combined with in vivo functional assays, transcriptomics, and molecular biology to dissect the tumor suppressor mechanisms that normally restrain this pathway.
