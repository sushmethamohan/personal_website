# Scientific Journey

### What Drives Me

My scientific journey has circled one core question: what goes wrong inside a cell when it stops listening to its own growth controls, and can that same failure point become a way to stop cancer?

It got personal early, after losing my grandmother to cancer. Since then, the pursuit of answering it has taken me through nanomedicine, brain tumor biology, and preclinical regulatory work, and taught me how much there is to gain from staying open to unexpected doors.

That pursuit has shaped me into a biologist with over 10 years of laboratory experience in neuroscience and cancer biology, working across mouse genetics, transcriptomics, and molecular biology, alongside nanoparticle engineering and immunology assays.

### Early Training

That pull started during my undergrad at SASTRA University in India, studying biotechnology and getting my first taste of research through work on plant-based nanoparticles and microbiology. It grew stronger during a research exchange at Harvard–MIT, where I worked on engineering supramolecular nanoparticles for immune modulation in cancer, my first real grounding in wet lab biochemistry, immunology, and basic molecular biology techniques. Watching a nanoparticle nudge an immune response in a specific direction is what made the question stop feeling abstract. I was passionate about bringing that same kind of precise intervention to a different problem, one in the developing brain, where the standard of care was still largely surgical resection, often leaving patients with lasting neurocognitive effects. It was clear better treatments were needed, and I wanted to understand the biology deeply enough to help get there.

### PhD Research

That's what brought me to my PhD in Neuroscience at McGill University, the years that ended up defining most of what I do now. I started with a rotation project on a cytokine receptor signaling pathway in glioblastoma, using in vitro assays and mouse xenograft models to test whether shutting the receptor down could suppress tumor growth, my first real exposure to how a single receptor pathway can tip a brain tumor toward aggression. It set the direction for everything that followed.

The core of my doctoral work became something I hadn't expected to find: an RNA decay pathway, one I'd have written off as cellular housekeeping, turned out to have a previously unrecognized role in medulloblastoma, the most common malignant pediatric brain tumor. Using mouse genetics, transcriptomics, and molecular biology, I traced how this pathway connects to Sonic Hedgehog signaling, the driver pathway in this cancer subtype. The work is currently in revision at *Developmental Cell*, and it's an important piece of work that shaped how I think about science today.

### Postdoc and Industry Experience

I stayed on at the Montreal Clinical Research Institute (IRCM) for a short postdoc to finish what I'd started. I'd put years into the medulloblastoma project and wanted to see it through to publication, and to help push the work in a deeper direction before moving on. I used the time to expand the project's scope for where the lab could take it next, exploring patient-derived mouse models, and beginning early testing and validation of an RNA-based therapeutic approach built on my PhD findings, both in vitro and in vivo.

Then I moved into industry, joining ImmuniT, a Montreal-based contract research organization (CRO), where I grew from Scientific Analyst to Study Director. I led a small team through the full lifecycle of immunotoxicity studies for sponsor clients' IND submissions, designing experiments, allocating tasks, working alongside the team at the bench, analyzing data, and preparing regulatory-ready reports for clients to submit to regulatory bodies (FDA, EMA, and equivalents). Along the way, I also helped refine the existing GLP-compliant platform we worked from. Academic training gave me scientific rigor, the ability to build an argument, test it, and defend it. ImmuniT added regulatory rigor on top of that, presenting data with the kind of traceability and precision a regulatory audit (FDA, EMA, and equivalents) demands.

### What's Next

I recently moved to Germany, and I'm actively looking for postdoctoral or industry scientist roles in neuroscience, cancer biology, RNA biology, or translational oncology. In the meantime, I'm building on that with self-directed training in computational analysis (R/Python), working through single-cell RNA-seq datasets one at a time to develop end-to-end analysis skills.

That mix of curiosity and precision, chasing the right question and backing it up with rigorous execution, is exactly what I want to bring to wherever I land next.
