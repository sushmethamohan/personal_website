# OSMR Signaling in Glioblastoma and Neural Stem Cells (PhD Rotation)

During a PhD rotation, I worked on understanding how a specific cytokine receptor signaling pathway drives glioblastoma, and whether that same pathway also shapes the behavior of the neural stem cells thought to give rise to these tumors. The lab had previously shown that OSMR (Oncostatin M receptor) partners with the EGFRvIII/STAT3 oncogenic pathway to drive the survival and growth of Brain Tumor Stem Cells (BTSC). My part of the rotation extended this question in two directions, one in established tumors and one in the normal stem cells that may precede them.

To ask whether directly shutting down OSMR in established tumors could suppress their growth, I helped generate and validate a doxycycline-inducible CRISPR interference (CRISPRi-dCas9) system to knock down OSMR in patient-derived BTSC in a controllable, reversible way, using flow cytometry to sort and establish the resulting stable cell lines. I tested this using subcutaneous xenograft mouse models, and used RT-qPCR to validate OSMR-regulated genes identified from RNA-seq data.

To ask whether this same pathway affects normal neural stem cells and not just tumor cells, I isolated adult neural stem cells from the mouse subventricular zone and cultured them using the neurosphere assay, confirming their identity by immunofluorescence staining. I used western blotting to characterize OSMR expression, and functional assays such as limiting dilution and cell viability assays to test how the OSMR ligand OSM affected self-renewal and proliferation in these cells. I also used electroporation-based transfection to over-express OSMR in these cells, to ask whether normal stem cells could be pushed toward a more tumor-like state.

Knocking down OSMR impaired tumor growth in vivo, supporting it as a viable therapeutic target in glioblastoma. This was a dense, technique-heavy rotation, mouse xenograft work, an inducible gene-editing system, and primary stem cell culture all at once, and it's where I first got comfortable with in vivo cancer models before starting my thesis work at McGill. I'm a co-author on a related commentary from this work, *"Targeting OSMR in glioma stem cells,"* published in *Oncotarget* in 2017 (DOI: 10.18632/oncotarget.15066).

**Skills:** In vivo subcutaneous xenograft models, CRISPRi-dCas9 inducible knockdown systems, RT-qPCR, neurosphere assay and primary neural stem cell culture, limiting dilution/sphere formation assays, electroporation, immunoblotting, immunofluorescence, cell viability/proliferation assays, flow cytometry/FACS, hypothesis-driven independent project design.
